Riccardo  a.k.a. [**Meru**](https://github.com/merumerutho) isn't just a prolific and experimental musician, he's a brillant coder and polyedric artist super active in the micromusic/visual worldwide scene (go and see [**LOVJ**](https://github.com/merumerutho/LOVJ)); Oh, he's also a good friend and a super kind person :3 

```interview
/////////////////////////

> STC: Hello there!
> MERU: 
Hey! Glad to be partecipating to this interview.

> STC: How did it start? How did you step into micromusic? Were you a fan/listener before performing it?
> MERU: 
I was barely a teenager when I first got in contact with the world of chiptune / micromusic. 
It all started when I first discovered Anamanaguchi's "Helix Nebula" on Youtube. This was in summer 2010, as I remember they were in the middle of releasing their series of Summer Singles.
At the time I barely understood how any of that was possible, and proceeded to dig in to figure out how any of that could be possible. I quickly became obsessed with the whole topic. 
After that I stumbled upon a video of maru303 busking with a gameboy on the streets of Japan. The deal was sealed, I was entranced. Whatever that was, I wanted to be a part of it.
My googling led me to 8bc, just one year before it closed, enough time to let me discover the reality of micromusic, keep me hooked and follow the community on other forums and platforms.
I figured chiptune music wasn't about being a genre rather than a medium itself, and the people into it were actually really diversified.
I lived these years mostly as a lurker but I remember timidly engaging with the community from time to time. My music was crap and I knew it. I looked for criticism, but moderately.
The whole view shifted in 2013 as I attended a show by Buskerdroid, Microman and Secret Lab. The venue was small but I figured out the huge difference between live shows and my "internet-centered" perception of chiptune music of the time.
Big changes came through when I went to uni in 2014, as I went to live within 1 hour or so from both Milan and Bologna. 
Kenobit had just started organizing the Milano Chiptune Underground nights, and I attended an amazing show at XM24 in Bologna with Toriena and NNNNNNNNN. 
I met some people from the italian community I had been in touch with for years, online-only, and some others who I didn't know.
Among them was IDecade, who invited me to play my first gig in Montebelluna. I still have the flyer. My music was still crap but I had finally found the real kick out of this whole music thing. 
I have always landed gigs through community support and eventually all that always mattered to me has always been the community thing itself rather than just go performing.
In this respect, my relationship with chiptune has been a well-maintained slow-burning fire which never extinguished; instead, it always found new wood to burn. Each gig has been a lesson learned and a chance to collect new inspiration and ideas.
I think what helped me through this journey is that I didn't rush through making music with the objective of performing it at first. For years I didn't even consider the possibility. I was just making music for the sake of it.
Later on, though, performing live has been the major boost to motivate me in making music. There's no comparison, the energy I feel in live gigs is unmatched. That's what most of my music lives for now.


> STC: What's your hardware/software specs, and why did you choose it?
> MERU: 
Apart from my very early days messing with GoatTracker2, I immediately jumped aboard the LSDJ bandwagon. It has been since 2010 up to around 2020, so around a decade of LSDJ tracking. So for years my setup has been 2xLSDJ on 2xDMG.
Eventually, despite my extreme attachment to LSDJ as a tool, since 2017 I started introducing nanoloop, and now my current setup is:
- one Nanoloop2 2.7.9 cart (on GBA);
- a nanoloop mono cart (on GBA as well)
- a Dirtywave M8
- a modular effect and mixer setup, mostly completed but still subject to changes.


> STC: Are there any other music instruments outside of the GameBoy? Were you born as electronic musician?
> MERU: 
My journey with music actually started before chiptune, as I was playing the electric guitar and was more into metal music. 
In my early experiments I tried blending in chiptune with metal music, inspired by Machinae Supremacy, but it never felt quite right.
Nowadays, while I still enjoy playing my guitar I'm definetely completely focused in electronic and chiptune music. 
The Dirtywave M8 in my setup has found its way a couple of years ago as the missing link, as it's the modern piece of gear that is the closest thing to LSDJ.


> STC: Why did you choose to approach this hybrid solution in your backline?  Did this somehow changed you way to create music?
> MERU: 
Before 2017, all my music was strictly chiptune, and it was really the classic LSDJ sound. At some point I felt this was creating a constraint in my intentions. 
I introduced nanoloop as a gateway, and it became my main platform for years. That changed a lot of things because the whole interface of nanoloop is completely different from LSDJ.
Nanoloop though allows me to write music as a free flow, which matches properly with my style of music, which is a bit uncoherent and chaotic at times. My first release is titled "Absolute lack of consistency" because of this reason. 
With the M8 eventually, I decided to break out of the "gameboy-only" paradigm.
The M8 (along with the modular effects) has been a proper way to introduce new elements and expand on the sound. This was really the motivation behind it.
Introducing the M8 was the only efficient way to include a "modern" element in the setup because it has the same interface of LSDJ by design. Trash80 really understood the need of people who were coming from a decade of LSDJ intensive use.
Opposite to nanoloop, the M8 felt like a comeback in terms of how I design my music because of the LSDJ similarity, and it brought back some way to actually structure real songs.


> STC: Do you provide any syncronizazion feature between your instruments (audio/midi-clock..)
> MERU:
While I write songs separately on these platforms, with a couple of exception (I tried doing songs using both NL2 and the M8 together), I do try to keep the devices synced at all times.
This way I can transition between songs easily during sets, and try out a bit of experimenting. The modular setup is also clock synced and the effects enhance each device in a different way, but it really isn't the central part of the performance.
For example, I do have filter+delay+reverb applied on Nanoloop2 to give it more room, and general compression to handle the dynamics and squash the devices together.
There's a couple of instances in which the M8 provides a sidechaining structural backbone while nanoloop does the rest of the song.
There are quite a few other experiments I'd like to explore. One of these is running a DJ setup to remix vocals from various songs with music produced in Nanoloop.
The key point is that this large setup was made with the sync idea in mind because it means I can try combining different ideas I've been cultivating separately, and build iteratively by adding small blocks to songs that are already working standalone.


> STC: Where your inspiration come from? Is there a particular artist that influenced your style?
> MERU: Among my other earliest influences were Anamanaguchi, Henry Homesweet, Sabrepulse, Shirobon, Bit Shifter, FighterX. These are now considerable sort of classics. 
Later on I guess both my intentions and the scene evolved, I'd say I got closer and closer to the bleepstreet records label as the most influencing: cTrix, Ultrasyd (RIP), but also others such as boaconstructor and Trey Frey.
Inevitably, closer artists have been an incredible influence in the latest years. Specifically, CPU Cake and the whole Square Terror Collective are a continuous source of inspiration and technical oriented discussions.
Among all, though, Henry Homesweet has probably been the most inspiring for one key aspect: he's among the most eclectic artists, and has explored several platform and genres (with several aliases). He really has never failed to deliver.
> STC: What's your idea of chiptune, and what does it mean to be chiptune fan/artist nowadays
> MERU: My idea of chiptune has shifted a lot through the years, as the rest of the world also evolved.
At the beginning I was really sold on the concept of chiptune "purism" (i.e. some sort of strict ruleset to define what is chiptune and what is not).
But similar to how the rest of the world is not black and white, I figured out chiptune is a largely sparse concept and part of the beauty of it is in its diversity, and the artists that can approach it from various angles.
A bit counterintuitively, my way of making chiptune music, has become to "hide" the gameboys from the crowd.
Most people in chiptune must have experienced the small crowd of people who during and after a show can't believe their eyes and ears: "Wow, you're making music with a gameboy? How does it work, did you hack it?" and such and such.
At first it was flattering gathering interest because of the unusual choice of instrument, but I think it gets old quite fast. You can ride this wave as long as you want, but don't rely on it. 
What I want is for people to have fun, dance and enjoy what they're listening to. Drawing them in because I'm playing a gameboy is sort of gimmicky. It's cool that people think it's cool, but ultimately I do it for the sake of the process. 

> STC: Time really flies, it was yesterday that we met up in Bologna at the Bleependence day @Circolo Dev (R.I.P.). After a while we invited you in the first edition of Terrore Quadrato and that's 3 years ago. What are your feelings about it? Have you noticed any difference during the progression of our festivals and in our attitude?
> MERU: I have vivid memories of these event. Specifically I remember the DSi anecdote, and I still think of my first attendance to the Terrore Quadrato event as yesterday. 
I have an incredibly precious memory of it because I remember how many friends from outside the scene were there attending it. The atmosphere was pure magic. The Terrore Quadrato gigs are all part of my most memorable core chiptune memories.
Since then I figured my attendance in playing festivals has declined slightly, but the quality of it has improved. I feel like I'm going at my own pace now, and I don't feel the same anxiety I used to have. 
I have grown to love my own production rather than constantly review it for mistakes and issues. And the latest gigs with Terrore Quadrato have constantly been a reconfirmation.

> STC: Have you ever played in a b2b set? It would be intresting to see you managing a combo session with another musician or dj.
> MERU: I have to admit I've never played in a b2b set. I feel like the first person in list would be Violante, as they're the one I could most easily practice with. 
I feel like this sort of thing requires a silent communication and mutual feeling skillset that is not something you can easily come up on the spot with, unless both people involved are incredibly used to it.
So I would definetely love to practice, and I think it would be interesting to come up with ways to incorporate someone else's style in a setlist.
```

```interview
> STC: Chiptune isn't cheap anymore, and as collective we believe that inflation of the retrogaming devices, semiconductor crisis and turbo-capitalism is making micromusic a less approachable discipline. Since a GameBoy DMG cost floats around 100 bucks, what is your prevision about the future of this genre? 
> MERU: 
I know many people still have the idea of "chiptune" strictly as "music from a chip", and that's fair.
But, reconnecting with what I said about "my way" of doing chiptune, while I mentioned "hiding my gameboys", I still do believe in the political message hidden in the act of recovering a "limited" old console to make music with. 
What's missing now is that we have to get along with times. For reasons beyound our control, Game Boys, Commodores, Amigas and other consoles from the 80s and 90s are no longer considered cheap scrap.
They're now collector items and their market price has eploded. While I wish it were a bubble, I'm afraid the perceived legacy we've been silently appreciating through chiptune has caught up with the general "pop culture" market.
Because of this reason, I suggest that whoever's interested in chiptune music as an upcycling technique should move on to newer platforms. 
Specifically, I suggested in the past the Nintendo DS. I have created a tracker player for DS, intended to be used as a DJ deck, called maxmxds (before known as xmxds). It's available on github.
Now I'm actively working on a more ambitious project to create a complete tracker for DS, which will be called maxtracker. This way I really hope to bring my own contribution to the future of the scene.

> STC: Can you tell us about your vjing software development? What is LOVJ?
> MERU:
LOVJ is a project I've been slowly working on for years now, culminating from my initial experiments with pico-8.
Pico-8 is a fantasy retro-console, which means it basically feels like a very limited platform for games (think of a hybrid between the NES and the GB) which is programmed in LUA.
I was amazed by newborn demoscene on the platform, and I decided to pick that up for creating vj loops to play at gigs. Fast forward several years and I figured out I could adopt the more powerful LOVE2D engine to recreate the same environment but expand on it with less limitations.
Kind of similarly to how my music journey evolved, I started integrating more and more modern features to LOVJ, and I revamped my interest in its development lately. 
At the beginning I just used to recreate some of the low-res pixel scenes from pico8, but eventually I went for more advanced "tech-demos" adding support to automatic modulation of parameters, shader support, a video player backend, Spout support (so it can be routed to other more professional tools), MIDI, OSC, and much more.
It's also supposed to be used also as a livecoding platform where one can edit patches in real-time. I do have even bigger plans for it, but I don't want to say too much. It's available for free on github, if anyone wants to try it.

> STC: Micromusic or microvisual, which one has the most active scene?
> MERU: 
First of all I'd like to clarify that I don't really see these two things as separate, the same way as artistic intent often transcends the medium, but I do recognize there are instances in which it's better to make distinctions.
Posing a comparison, micromusic is certainly a more recognized term associated with the chiptune scene, while "microvisual" is probably more like a sort of umbrella term I've been using to collect various concepts associated with micromusic but in the visual domain.
This includes, but is not limited to, glitch-bent consoles that generate glitchy visuals, the use of demos or retro "demo-like" software to generate video from old consoles, software like glitchNES or glitchGBA, ASCII and PETSCII art, rom-hacks and tileset art, and also more modern ways to create visuals with a "micro" feel.
I think ideally a way to bridge a sort of gap with the micromusic world, and grow the scene and interest in microvisual, would be a powerful VJ program for a retro console, sort of "the LSDJ" for visuals. There's a bunch of things out there already, actually, specifically old commercial software for PS1.
I'd prefer these were handheld. INFU AV for example mitigated this by using PS1 VJ software emulating it on a mini pc. Personally I always think about "pikilipita pikix", a very obscure piece of VJ software for the GP2X platform which best incarnates my idea of microvisual. 
Information on it can somehow still be found online, but I feel like it's too obscure, and a potential successor would be pretty great for the scene.
I do recognize, however, that an interesting "microvisual italy" scene has been shaping up for a while now, for example with the works of ARTEMISIA and Marbit who've been excellently creating visual art for a long time. I hope this visual part of the scene gets the right recognition.

> STC: Can you smell the fear of losing interest in chiptune hardware? One day you wake up and you'll be surrounded by modular patches with no TEENSY or GBA. How strong is your love for what you do and how long will it last?
> MERU: Not at all. As mentioned, I'm too deep in my current process, which is all about making music with a device in my hands. Without it, I feel lost. Time spent in front of a computer is just boring to me. That's why I mitigated the issue by putting a computer in my modular system. 
It's something that I definetely need at some point, but I prefer to keep it constrained to the realm of music only. This avoids any form of distraction.
> STC: It's time for us to shut up and let you go! It was a pleasure. See you around, world is micro!
> MERU: It's been my honor. I hope to see you soon and to keep growing the scene, and growing IN the scene.
```
